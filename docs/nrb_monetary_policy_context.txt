NRB Monetary Policy Context for NEPSE
=====================================

This file explains how Nepal Rastra Bank’s (NRB) monetary policy works
and how it typically affects NEPSE, especially banking and interest-
sensitive sectors.

1. What is Monetary Policy?
---------------------------
Monetary policy is the framework and set of tools used by Nepal Rastra
Bank (NRB) to manage liquidity, interest rates, inflation, and overall
financial stability in the economy.

Key objectives include:
- Maintaining price stability (controlling inflation).
- Supporting sustainable economic growth.
- Preserving financial sector stability and adequate liquidity.

2. Main Policy Tools NRB Uses
-----------------------------
NRB uses several instruments to influence interest rates and liquidity.

a) Policy Rate / Repo Rate
--------------------------
- The policy rate (often the repo rate) is the benchmark short-term
  interest rate that NRB uses to inject liquidity into the banking
  system via repo operations.
- When NRB raises the policy rate:
  - Borrowing costs for banks increase.
  - Lending rates to businesses and households tend to rise.
  - Credit growth can slow, and economic activity may cool.
- When NRB reduces the policy rate:
  - Banks can access cheaper funds.
  - Lending rates may fall.
  - Credit growth and investment may pick up.

b) Interest Rate Corridor
-------------------------
NRB usually sets an “interest rate corridor” with three key rates:
- Standing Liquidity Facility (SLF) rate:
  - Upper bound; the rate at which banks can borrow overnight from NRB.
- Policy / Repo rate:
  - Middle of the corridor; the main policy signal.
- Standing Deposit Facility (SDF) or similar rate:
  - Lower bound; rate at which banks can deposit surplus funds at NRB.

This corridor guides interbank market rates and indirectly influences
deposit and lending rates of commercial banks.

c) Cash Reserve Ratio (CRR) and Statutory Liquidity Ratio (SLR)
---------------------------------------------------------------
- CRR:
  - The minimum percentage of deposits that banks must maintain as cash
    reserves with NRB.
- SLR:
  - The minimum percentage of deposits that banks must hold in liquid
    assets (such as government securities) in addition to CRR.

When NRB raises CRR/SLR:
- Banks have less money to lend.
- Liquidity tightens and interest rates can rise.

When NRB lowers CRR/SLR:
- Banks have more lendable funds.
- Liquidity improves and lending rates may ease.

d) Open Market Operations (OMOs)
--------------------------------
- NRB conducts repo, reverse repo, deposit collection, and other
  operations to inject or absorb liquidity as needed.
- Frequent OMOs smooth out short-term liquidity swings without always
  changing the headline policy rate.

3. How Monetary Policy Affects NEPSE
------------------------------------

a) Banking and Financial Stocks
-------------------------------
- Policy rate hikes, tighter CRR/SLR, or liquidity-absorbing actions
  can:
  - Increase funding costs for banks.
  - Compress net interest margins if lending rates cannot be raised as
    quickly.
  - Slow credit growth and profitability.
- Policy easing (rate cuts, liquidity injections) can:
  - Improve loan growth.
  - Support lower deposit and lending rates.
  - Boost earnings if asset quality is stable.
Because commercial banks form a large portion of NEPSE’s market
capitalization, NRB decisions often move the overall index.

b) Interest-Sensitive Sectors
-----------------------------
- Sectors relying on debt financing (like hydropower or manufacturing)
  are affected by borrowing costs.
- Higher interest rates make new projects more expensive and can reduce
  valuations; lower rates do the opposite.
- When NRB adopts an expansionary policy, these sectors may benefit
  from cheaper credit and improved demand.

c) Investor Sentiment and Liquidity
-----------------------------------
- Aggressive tightening (sharp rate hikes, strict credit controls) may
  reduce risk appetite and lead to selling pressure in NEPSE.
- Easing measures (rate cuts, relaxed credit conditions) often improve
  sentiment and risk-taking, supporting equity inflows.
- Monetary policy reviews and mid-term updates can trigger short-term
  volatility as investors adjust expectations.

4. Typical Questions the AI Should Answer Using This Context
------------------------------------------------------------
- “Why are banking stocks falling after NRB’s latest policy?”
  - Possible reasons:
    - Policy rate hike.
    - Higher CRR or stricter lending rules.
    - Concerns about asset quality or slower credit growth.
- “How does a policy rate cut affect NEPSE?”
  - Possible effects:
    - Cheaper credit.
    - Improved bank margins (if deposit rates adjust appropriately).
    - Higher valuations for interest-sensitive sectors.
- “What does the interest rate corridor mean for NEPSE?”
  - It shows the range within which short-term rates are expected to
    move, influencing banks’ funding costs and, indirectly, stock
    valuations.

5. Using NRB Policy in Models and Explanations
----------------------------------------------
For a NEPSE-focused AI and model pipeline:

- Tag major NRB policy announcements (new monetary policy, quarterly
  reviews) along the time axis of data.
- When explaining big moves in banking and financial stocks, check
  whether a policy change occurred around that period.
- Consider using simple macro features (for example, policy rate level,
  direction of last policy change) as additional inputs for long-horizon
  models, especially for sector-level forecasts or risk analysis.

6. Limitations
--------------
- Monetary policy is only one of many drivers; political events,
  regulatory changes, global markets, and domestic credit conditions
  also matter.
- The impact of a given NRB decision can be delayed or muted depending
  on transmission through the banking system.
- Historical relationships between NRB policy and NEPSE performance may
  change over time as the economy and market structure evolve.

NEPSE Trading FAQs
==================

This file contains common questions NEPSE investors ask and concise,
consistent answers for the AI to use.

1. What are the trading hours in NEPSE?
---------------------------------------
NEPSE’s regular trading session typically runs from 11:00 AM to 3:00 PM
on working trading days (currently Sunday to Thursday or Sunday to Friday,
depending on official notifications). There is usually a pre-open session
before 11:00 AM for opening price discovery and a short closing phase
near 3:00 PM for final price formation. Official circulars should be
checked for the latest exact timings and holiday lists.

2. What is T+2 settlement?
--------------------------
T+2 means trades are fully settled two working days after the trade date.

- On T (trade day), buy and sell orders match on NEPSE.
- On T+1, buyers must ensure funds are available with their brokers, and
  sellers must authorize share delivery (e.g., via E-DIS).
- On T+2, shares are credited to buyers’ Demat accounts and funds are
  credited to sellers’ bank accounts.

3. Can I sell shares I bought today?
------------------------------------
In a delivery-based market like NEPSE, you normally need to receive
shares into your Demat account before you can sell them. Same-day buy
and sell (pure intraday trading) is generally not allowed unless you
already had deliverable shares to sell. In practice, you cannot rely on
“buy today, sell today” like in some other markets.

4. Can I do short selling in NEPSE?
-----------------------------------
No. Short selling (selling borrowed shares you do not own) is not allowed
in NEPSE under current regulations. All trades are effectively
delivery-based. To sell, you must hold the shares in your Demat account
or acquire them in time for settlement.

5. How do circuit breakers and price bands work?
-----------------------------------------------
Circuit breakers protect the market from extreme intraday moves.

- Daily Price Band:
  - Each stock has a maximum daily price movement limit (for example,
    a percentage range around the previous close). If the stock reaches
    the upper or lower limit, it cannot trade beyond that price.
- Index-Based Circuit Breaker:
  - The NEPSE index has intraday thresholds (for example, a halt if it
    moves beyond a certain percentage within a specified time, and
    full-day closure if a higher threshold is reached).
Exact percentages, time windows, and differences by stock type (e.g.,
equity vs mutual funds) are defined in official NEPSE/SEBON circulars and
may change over time, so they must be checked from official sources.

6. How do I read the NEPSE floorsheet?
--------------------------------------
The floorsheet is a trade report showing executed transactions.

Common columns include:
- Symbol: The stock’s trading symbol.
- Buyer / Seller Broker: Broker IDs of parties to the trade.
- Quantity: Number of shares traded.
- Rate: Trade price per share.
- Amount: Total traded value (Rate × Quantity).
Floorsheet analysis helps investors see which brokers are active on the
buy or sell side and whether large trades are happening at specific
price levels.

7. What do LTP, 52-Week High/Low, and WACC mean?
------------------------------------------------
- LTP (Last Traded Price):
  - The most recent executed trade price for a stock.
- 52-Week High / 52-Week Low:
  - The highest and lowest prices at which the stock has traded in the
    last one year (approximate 52-week period).
- WACC (Weighted Average Cost of Capital):
  - A finance term (used more in valuation than daily trading) that
    measures the average return a company must pay its capital providers
    (debt and equity). It is not a NEPSE-specific indicator but can be
    used in fundamental valuation analysis.

8. What are the broker commission rates?
----------------------------------------
Broker commission in NEPSE is charged according to slab-based rates
approved by SEBON.

- For small trades, the percentage commission is higher.
- As trade size increases, the commission percentage typically decreases.
- In addition to broker commission, investors pay NEPSE transaction fees,
  SEBON regulatory fees, and DP/settlement charges.
Exact current slabs and rates should be checked from SEBON or broker
websites, as they can change over time.

9. What is the difference between the MeroShare app and broker TMS?
-------------------------------------------------------------------
- MeroShare:
  - Provided by CDSC via Depository Participants (DPs).
  - Used mainly for Demat management, applying for IPO/FPO/rights
    through ASBA, viewing portfolio and transaction history, and
    handling E-DIS for share delivery.
- Broker TMS (Trading Management System):
  - Provided by individual brokers.
  - Used to place buy/sell orders, view live market depth, trading
    status, and execute trades on NEPSE.
In simple terms, MeroShare is your Demat and IPO portal, while TMS is
your actual trading platform.

10. Why do technical indicators sometimes give false signals in NEPSE?
----------------------------------------------------------------------
NEPSE has characteristics that can confuse indicators:

- Low liquidity and irregular volume in many scrips.
- Sudden jumps due to news, corporate actions, or circuit limits.
- Gaps caused by book closures, dividends, or bonus/rights adjustments.

Because of this, indicators like RSI, MACD, and Bollinger Bands should
be combined with volume, corporate action context, and sector sentiment
instead of being used blindly.

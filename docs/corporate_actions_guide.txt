NEPSE Corporate Actions Guide
=============================

This file explains common corporate actions in NEPSE and how they affect
prices, charts, and technical indicators (important for both traders and AI).

1. Bonus Shares
---------------
Bonus shares are free additional shares issued to existing shareholders
out of accumulated profits or reserves.

- Example:
  - 20% bonus means you receive 20 new shares for every 100 shares held.
- Price Adjustment on Ex-Date:
  - On the ex-bonus date, the theoretical price is adjusted downward so
    that total market value remains roughly the same (ignoring sentiment).
  - Approximate adjusted price:
    - Adjusted Price ≈ Old Price / (1 + Bonus %)
    - Example: Old Price = Rs 1,000, Bonus = 20% → Adjusted Price ≈ 1,000 / 1.2 ≈ Rs 833.
- Investor Impact:
  - Number of shares increases; per-share price drops.
  - Total investment value stays nearly unchanged in theory, but market
    reaction can be positive or negative.
- NEPSE / CDSC Announcements:
  - Bonus decisions are announced through company notices on NEPSE’s
    website and CDSC / DP notifications.
- AI / Indicator Impact:
  - Historical prices should be adjusted for bonus events; otherwise EMA,
    RSI, MACD, and other indicators will show artificial gaps.
  - When backtesting, always use adjusted data so that signals around
    the bonus date are meaningful.

2. Rights Shares
----------------
Rights shares give existing shareholders the right to buy additional
shares, usually at a discount, within a fixed time period and ratio.

- Example:
  - 1:2 rights at Rs 100 means you can buy 1 new share for every 2 shares
    you hold, paying Rs 100 per rights share.
- Theoretical Ex-Rights Price (TERP) Formula:
  - Suppose:
    - P = Cum-rights market price before rights.
    - N = Number of existing shares.
    - R = Rights price (issue price for rights shares).
    - M = Number of new rights shares offered.
  - Then:
    - TERP ≈ (P × N + R × M) / (N + M)
  - This gives the approximate theoretical price after rights issue.
- Price Behavior:
  - When rights are announced, price reacts based on dilution, discount
    level, and sentiment.
  - Around the ex-rights date, price can gap down towards TERP.
- NEPSE / CDSC Notices:
  - Rights issue details (ratio, price, book closure date, payment
    instructions) are published on NEPSE and CDSC portals and conveyed
    through DPs.
- AI / Indicator Impact:
  - Price and volume around rights issue dates can be erratic.
  - Historical data should be adjusted for rights factor; otherwise
    indicators will treat the mechanical price change as a real trend
    reversal.

3. Cash Dividends
-----------------
Cash dividends are cash payments made to shareholders out of profits.

- Example:
  - 20% cash dividend on a Rs 100 face value share = Rs 20 per share.
- Key Dates:
  - Book Closure / Record Date:
    - Shareholders on record by this date are eligible for the dividend.
  - Ex-Dividend Date:
    - On or around this date, the stock trades without dividend
      entitlement; price often gaps down by roughly the dividend amount.
- Dividend Yield in Nepal Context:
  - Dividend Yield (%) ≈ (Annual Cash Dividend per Share / Current Market Price) × 100
  - Example:
    - Dividend = Rs 20 per share, Current Price = Rs 800 →
      Yield ≈ (20 / 800) × 100 = 2.5%.
- AI / Indicator Impact:
  - RSI, MACD, and other indicators may show a sharp drop on the
    ex-dividend candle; the system should recognize this as a dividend
    adjustment, not pure selling pressure.
  - For long-term analysis, adjusted close data including dividends gives
    a more realistic performance picture.

4. Stock Splits and Face Value Changes
--------------------------------------
A stock split increases the number of shares by reducing the face value
proportionally.

- Example:
  - 1:10 split (from Rs 100 to Rs 10 face value) multiplies share count
    by 10 and divides the price by 10.
- Reasons:
  - Improve liquidity.
  - Make the stock “look cheaper” to small investors.
- AI / Indicator Impact:
  - Like bonus shares, splits require full historical price and volume
    adjustment.
  - Without adjustment, long-term charts will show large artificial gaps
    and break trend indicators.

5. Book Closure and Record Dates
--------------------------------
Book closure is the period when a company closes its shareholder register
to determine eligible shareholders for dividends, bonuses, or rights.

- Practical Impact:
  - Investors must hold shares before the book closure / record date to
    be eligible for the announced benefit.
  - Trading volume can rise before book closure as investors try to
    qualify for dividends or bonuses.
- How Events Are Announced:
  - Book closure and record dates are published:
    - On the official NEPSE website (company news / notices).
    - Through CDSC and Depository Participants to Demat holders.
- AI / Indicator Impact:
  - Sudden spikes in volume before book closure and price adjustments
    afterwards are normal; models should not treat them purely as
    bullish or bearish events.

6. Special Events: Mergers, Acquisitions, and Delistings
--------------------------------------------------------
Companies may merge, be acquired, or get delisted.

- Mergers / Acquisitions:
  - Shareholders might receive shares of the new merged entity or cash,
    based on approved swap ratios.
  - Trading can be volatile around announcements and approvals.
- Delisting:
  - Shares are removed from NEPSE; investors may need to exit before
    delisting or follow specific exit procedures.
- AI / Indicator Impact:
  - For backtesting, data around such events should be tagged as special
    and sometimes excluded from generic strategy evaluation.
  - The system should avoid giving long-term trading signals for
    companies under confirmed delisting or merger into another entity.

7. How to Treat Corporate Actions in NEPSE AI
---------------------------------------------
For a NEPSE-focused AI system:

- Always prefer adjusted historical price data (accounting for bonus,
  rights, splits, and dividends) in backtests and indicator calculations.
- Tag important corporate action dates in metadata so retrieval and
  explanations can clearly mention why price/volume behaved unusually.
- When answering user queries, clearly distinguish between:
  - “Price fell because of dividend or rights adjustment” and
  - “Price fell because of negative sentiment or heavy selling.”

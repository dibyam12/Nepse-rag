KEY METRICS FOR HYDROPOWER STOCKS — COMPLETE GUIDE
=============================================================================

1. INSTALLED CAPACITY (MW)
   The nameplate capacity of the power project in megawatts.
   Larger capacity = more revenue potential.
   Upper Tamakoshi (UPPER): 456 MW — largest single project in Nepal.
   Most projects: 2-50 MW range. Projects above 100 MW are large.

2. ACTUAL GENERATION (GWh — Gigawatt Hours)
   Real electricity produced annually. Can be lower than theoretical max.
   Seasonal variation: Wet season (June-November) = high generation.
   Dry season (Dec-May) = low generation. Critical risk for run-of-river projects.
   Reservoir projects have storage — more stable year-round generation.

3. PLANT LOAD FACTOR (PLF)
   Formula: (Actual Generation ÷ Maximum Possible Generation) × 100
   Measures operational efficiency.
   Strong PLF: above 55% | Average: 40-55% | Weak: below 40%
   Run-of-river plants typically have lower PLF due to seasonal variation.
   Reservoir/storage projects have higher, more stable PLF.

4. POWER PURCHASE AGREEMENT (PPA)
   Contract with Nepal Electricity Authority (NEA) for buying electricity.
   KEY TERMS TO CHECK:
   - Duration: 25-35 years typical
   - Tariff rate (Rs per kWh): Wet season vs dry season pricing
   - Price escalation clause: Annual inflation adjustment in tariff
   - Take-or-pay clause: NEA pays even if grid can't absorb power
   Companies with inflation-adjusted, long-term PPAs = highest cash flow certainty.

5. COMMERCIAL OPERATION DATE (COD)
   Date when the project started selling electricity.
   Pre-COD companies: No revenue yet, pure speculation.
   Post-COD companies: Revenue generating, can be analyzed fundamentally.
   IMPORTANT: Many NEPSE-listed hydropower companies are pre-COD or early stage.
   Do NOT apply traditional P/E analysis to pre-COD companies.

6. DEBT-TO-EQUITY RATIO (D/E)
   Hydropower is capital-intensive: projects financed 70% debt, 30% equity typically.
   Moderate D/E (2:1 to 4:1) is normal for hydropower.
   High D/E above 5:1 = financial pressure risk, especially during low output.
   Interest burden during construction and early operation is significant.

7. NET INTEREST MARGIN / DEBT SERVICE COVERAGE RATIO (DSCR)
   DSCR = Operating Cash Flow ÷ Total Debt Service
   DSCR above 1.2 = comfortable | Below 1.0 = cannot service debt from operations
   Critical during dry season when cash flow is lowest.

8. NEA PAYMENT RISK
   NEA (Nepal Electricity Authority) is the sole buyer in Nepal.
   NEA payment delays are common — creates cash flow risk for smaller projects.
   Larger, older projects have better payment track records.
   Electricity exports to India diversify revenue but require infrastructure.

HOW TO RESPOND TO HYDROPOWER QUERIES
=============================================================================

For POST-COD (revenue generating) hydropower stocks:
1. State project capacity (MW) and operational status
2. Quote LTP, 52W range, volume
3. Mention EPS if available (many small hydros have thin margins)
4. P/E context: Hydro P/E varies wildly — 15x to 100x+ is normal
5. Key question: Wet vs dry season generation profile
6. Mention PPA terms if known

For PRE-COD (construction phase) stocks:
1. Clearly state "pre-commercial operation — no revenue yet"
2. Valuation is speculative, based on future expected earnings
3. Key risk: construction delays, cost overruns, NEA tariff approval
4. Do NOT give P/E or EPS — these don't apply

SEASONAL PATTERN (for all run-of-river plants):
  June-November: High water flow = high generation = high revenue
  December-May: Low water flow = reduced generation = cash flow pressure
  NEPSE hydropower stocks tend to rise in anticipation of wet season.

SECTOR VALUATION:
  P/E: 20x-60x for quality operating plants
  P/E: N/A or 100x+ for early-stage or pre-COD companies
  P/B: 2x-5x typical

NEPSE Sector Descriptions
=========================

This file summarizes the main NEPSE sectors, their typical business activities,
and how they often behave in the market context.

Commercial Banks
----------------
Class “A” banks licensed by Nepal Rastra Bank (NRB). They are the largest and
most systemically important financial institutions in Nepal, providing retail
and corporate banking, loans, deposits, remittances, and trade finance.
Their earnings and valuations are heavily influenced by interest rate policy,
credit growth, non-performing loans, and NRB regulations. Large bank stocks
often dominate NEPSE index movements.

Development Banks
-----------------
Class “B” financial institutions focusing on development-oriented banking.
They provide project financing, SME loans, and regional banking services,
often with a focus on specific provinces or sectors. Development banks are
smaller than commercial banks but can show higher growth and more volatility.
Their performance is sensitive to NRB capital requirements and local economic activity.

Finance Companies
-----------------
Class “C” financial institutions that offer hire purchase, term loans, consumer
finance, and various credit facilities. They are typically smaller and more
specialized than commercial or development banks. Finance company stocks
can be more volatile, especially when liquidity cycles or regulatory changes
affect their business models and risk profiles.

Microfinance
------------
Microfinance institutions (MFIs) provide small loans, savings products, and
financial services to low-income and rural clients. They focus on financial
inclusion and group lending models. Microfinance stocks are known for high
beta and sharp rallies or corrections, influenced by NRB directives, interest
rate caps, and political or social policy changes.

Hydropower
----------
Companies involved in hydroelectric power generation, transmission, or related
infrastructure. Many are project-based, with long construction phases followed
by revenue streams from Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs). Hydropower stocks
are highly sensitive to project completion news, PPA terms, monsoon conditions,
and government energy policy. This sector often shows thematic rallies when
investor sentiment turns optimistic.

Insurance (Life)
----------------
Life insurance companies regulated by Beema Samiti. They provide life insurance,
endowment policies, term plans, and other long-term protection/savings products.
Key drivers include premium growth, persistency ratios, investment returns,
and regulatory reserving requirements. Life insurers may show long-term
growth potential but can be volatile around bonus/share announcements and
regulatory changes.

Insurance (Non-Life)
--------------------
Non-life (general) insurance companies provide products such as motor, fire,
marine, health, and property insurance. Their earnings depend on underwriting
discipline, claims ratios, and investment income. Non-life insurers often react
to major events (natural disasters, changes in motor insurance rules) and
adjust their pricing and reserves accordingly.

Manufacturing & Processing
--------------------------
Companies engaged in industrial production, such as cement, steel, food and
beverages, pharmaceuticals, and other processed goods. Their performance is
linked to construction demand, input costs (e.g., raw materials, energy),
exchange rates, and overall economic growth. Stocks in this sector may be
cyclical and sensitive to infrastructure spending and industrial policy.

Hotels & Tourism
----------------
Listed hotels, resorts, and tourism-related companies serving domestic and
international travelers. Their revenue is driven by tourist arrivals, room
occupancy rates, average room prices, and tourism policy. External shocks
(e.g., pandemics, natural disasters, geopolitical issues) can significantly
impact this sector, leading to high earnings volatility.

Trading
-------
Companies involved primarily in trading activities, such as importing and
distributing goods, consumer products, or industrial inputs. Their margins
depend on import costs, currency fluctuations, and competitive dynamics.
Trading companies can be sensitive to customs policies, tax changes, and
changes in consumer or industrial demand.

Investment
----------
Investment companies, holding companies, and entities that primarily invest
in other securities or projects. Their reported earnings often reflect
changes in the value of their investment portfolios, dividend income, and
realized/unrealized gains. These stocks may track broader market trends but
can trade at discounts or premiums to their underlying net asset value (NAV).

Others
------
Companies that do not neatly fit into the above categories. This may include
specialized service providers, technology-related firms, conglomerates with
diverse operations, and niche businesses. Their behavior varies widely
depending on individual business models, governance, and growth prospects.

(Optionally) Mutual Funds / Collective Investment
-------------------------------------------------
Although not always grouped as a separate “sector” in older classifications,
mutual funds and collective investment schemes (CIS) listed on NEPSE invest
in diversified portfolios of equities and fixed-income securities. Their
market prices can trade at discounts or premiums to NAV and are driven by
overall market sentiment, fund performance, and investor demand for diversified exposure.

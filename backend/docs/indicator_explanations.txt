Technical Indicator Explanations for NEPSE Stock Market
=========================================================

This document is a comprehensive reference for all major technical indicators used in
stock market analysis, specifically tailored to the Nepal Stock Exchange (NEPSE).
Each section includes: definition, formula, typical settings, interpretation guide,
practical usage examples, and NEPSE-specific edge cases.

Technical indicators are mathematical calculations based on price, volume, or open interest
data. They help traders and investors identify trends, momentum, volatility, and potential
buy/sell signals. They are classified into the following categories:

Categories of Technical Indicators:
  1. Trend Indicators — Identify the direction and strength of a trend (EMA, SMA, ADX, Ichimoku)
  2. Momentum Indicators — Measure the speed of price changes (RSI, MACD, Stochastic, CCI, ROC, Williams %R)
  3. Volatility Indicators — Measure the degree of price variation (Bollinger Bands, ATR, BBW, Keltner Channels)
  4. Volume Indicators — Analyze trading volume to confirm trends (OBV, VWAP, MFI, A/D Line, CMF)
  5. Market Sensitivity — Measure a stock's relationship to the broader market (Beta)
  6. Support & Resistance — Identify key price levels (Pivot Points, Fibonacci Retracement)

1. RSI (Relative Strength Index)
---------------------------------
Category: Momentum Indicator
Developed by: J. Welles Wilder Jr. (1978)

Definition:
RSI is a momentum oscillator that measures the speed and magnitude of recent price changes
to evaluate overbought or oversold conditions. It oscillates between 0 and 100.

Formula:
  RSI = 100 - [100 / (1 + RS)]
  RS (Relative Strength) = Average Gain over N periods / Average Loss over N periods

  Step-by-step:
  1. Calculate daily price changes (Close today - Close yesterday)
  2. Separate gains (positive changes) and losses (absolute value of negative changes)
  3. Calculate the average gain and average loss over N periods (typically 14)
  4. RS = Average Gain / Average Loss
  5. RSI = 100 - (100 / (1 + RS))

  For subsequent periods, use a smoothed average:
  Average Gain = [(Previous Average Gain × 13) + Current Gain] / 14
  Average Loss = [(Previous Average Loss × 13) + Current Loss] / 14

Typical Settings:
  - Period: 14 days (standard on NEPSE daily charts)
  - Range: 0 to 100
  - Some traders also use 9-period (more sensitive) or 21-period (smoother)

Interpretation:
  - Overbought zone: RSI > 70 — Price may be stretched upward; potential reversal or consolidation.
  - Oversold zone: RSI < 30 — Price may be stretched downward; potential bounce or reversal.
  - Neutral zone: RSI between 30 and 70 — No extreme signal.
  - RSI = 50: Centerline; RSI above 50 generally means upward momentum, below 50 means downward.
  - Bullish Divergence: Price makes a lower low but RSI makes a higher low — downtrend losing strength.
  - Bearish Divergence: Price makes a higher high but RSI makes a lower high — uptrend losing strength.

How Traders Use RSI:
  - Entry signal: Buy when RSI crosses above 30 (exiting oversold). Sell when RSI crosses below 70 (exiting overbought).
  - Trend confirmation: In an uptrend, RSI tends to stay between 40 and 90. In a downtrend, 10 to 60.
  - Divergence trading: Look for RSI divergences at key support/resistance levels.

Example:
  If NABIL has RSI = 28 after a 10-day decline, it indicates the stock may be oversold
  and due for a short-term bounce. However, confirm with volume and support levels before buying.

NEPSE Edge Cases:
  - In strong bull runs (e.g., hydropower or microfinance rallies), RSI can stay above 70 for weeks;
    treating every overbought reading as a sell signal can cause premature exits.
  - Many illiquid NEPSE stocks have flat days or sporadic large moves, which can create noisy RSI values.
  - Circuit limits (±10% daily band) can push RSI to extremes quickly; a single limit-up or limit-down
    day can distort the oscillator for several sessions.

2. MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence)
-------------------------------------------------
Category: Momentum / Trend Indicator
Developed by: Gerald Appel (1970s)

Definition:
MACD is a trend-following momentum indicator that shows the relationship between two
Exponential Moving Averages (EMAs) of a stock's price. It helps identify changes in the
strength, direction, momentum, and duration of a trend.

Formula:
  MACD Line = EMA(12) - EMA(26)
  Signal Line = EMA(9) of the MACD Line
  MACD Histogram = MACD Line - Signal Line

  Where:
  - EMA(12) = 12-period Exponential Moving Average of closing prices
  - EMA(26) = 26-period Exponential Moving Average of closing prices
  - The Signal Line smooths the MACD Line to generate clearer trading signals

Typical Settings:
  - Fast EMA: 12 periods
  - Slow EMA: 26 periods
  - Signal Line: 9-period EMA of MACD Line
  - Some traders use (8, 17, 9) for more sensitivity or (5, 35, 5) for longer-term trends

Interpretation:
  - Bullish Crossover: MACD Line crosses above Signal Line → momentum turning up (buy signal).
  - Bearish Crossover: MACD Line crosses below Signal Line → momentum turning down (sell signal).
  - Zero-Line Cross: MACD moving from negative to positive → trend shifting to bullish (and vice versa).
  - Histogram increasing: Momentum strengthening in the current direction.
  - Histogram decreasing: Momentum weakening; possible trend reversal ahead.
  - MACD Divergence: Similar to RSI — price and MACD moving in opposite directions signals potential reversal.

How Traders Use MACD:
  - Signal-line crossover: Most common signal. Buy on bullish crossover, sell on bearish crossover.
  - Zero-line crossover: More conservative; confirms trend direction change.
  - Histogram analysis: Watch for histogram shrinking before a crossover to get early warning.
  - Combine with RSI: MACD bullish crossover + RSI exiting oversold = stronger buy signal.

Example:
  If NICA's MACD Line is at +3.2 and the Signal Line is at +1.8, the MACD Histogram is +1.4
  (bullish). If the histogram starts shrinking (e.g., +1.0, +0.5), momentum is weakening
  even though MACD is still positive.

NEPSE Edge Cases:
  - Many NEPSE stocks trend slowly then jump sharply; MACD can lag during sudden breakouts.
  - Sideways or low-volume phases generate many small crossovers, leading to whipsaws.
  - Circuit-limit days can create large gaps that make MACD histogram spike temporarily.

3. EMA (Exponential Moving Average)
-------------------------------------
Category: Trend Indicator

Definition:
EMA is a type of moving average that gives more weight to recent prices, making it more
responsive to new information than the Simple Moving Average (SMA). It smooths price data
to help identify trend direction and potential support/resistance levels.

Formula:
  EMA_today = (Price_today × Multiplier) + (EMA_yesterday × (1 - Multiplier))
  Multiplier (α) = 2 / (N + 1)

  Where N is the number of periods.
  Example for EMA-20: α = 2 / (20 + 1) = 0.0952

Comparison with SMA:
  SMA = Sum of N closing prices / N
  - SMA gives equal weight to all prices; EMA gives more weight to recent prices.
  - EMA reacts faster to price changes; SMA is smoother but slower.

Common Periods on NEPSE:
  - EMA-9 or EMA-10: Very short-term trend (scalping, day trading).
  - EMA-20: Short-term trend (about one trading month on NEPSE which trades ~22 days/month).
  - EMA-50: Medium-term trend (about 2.5 months).
  - EMA-100: Intermediate trend.
  - EMA-200: Long-term trend / primary trend filter.

Interpretation:
  - Price above EMA: Bullish bias; the stock is in an uptrend relative to that period.
  - Price below EMA: Bearish bias; the stock is in a downtrend relative to that period.
  - EMA acting as support: In uptrends, price often bounces off the EMA (buy-the-dip zone).
  - EMA acting as resistance: In downtrends, price often reverses at the EMA.
  - Golden Cross: Short EMA (e.g., 20) crosses above longer EMA (e.g., 50) → bullish trend signal.
  - Death Cross: Short EMA crosses below longer EMA → bearish trend signal.

How Traders Use EMA:
  - Trend identification: Check if price is above or below key EMAs.
  - Dynamic support/resistance: Trade bounces off EMA lines.
  - Crossover systems: Buy on Golden Cross, sell on Death Cross.
  - Multiple EMAs: Use 20, 50, and 200 EMAs together — when all three are aligned (price > 20 > 50 > 200), it's a strong uptrend.

Example:
  If NABIL is trading at 540 with EMA-20 at 530 and EMA-50 at 510, the stock is above both
  EMAs, indicating a bullish trend. The EMA-20 at 530 acts as the first support level.

NEPSE Edge Cases:
  - Illiquid stocks with frequent gaps can make EMAs less smooth and more prone to sudden flips.
  - Corporate actions (bonus shares, right issues, stock splits) can distort past prices if not adjusted.
  - Very slow-moving scrips may have flat EMAs that add little insight.

4. SMA (Simple Moving Average)
-------------------------------
Category: Trend Indicator

Definition:
SMA calculates the arithmetic mean of a stock's closing prices over a specified number of periods.
It is the simplest form of a moving average and provides a smoothed line to identify trends.

Formula:
  SMA = (P1 + P2 + P3 + ... + PN) / N
  Where P1 to PN are the closing prices for the past N periods.

Common Periods:
  - SMA-10: Very short term
  - SMA-20: Short term (1 month)
  - SMA-50: Medium term (about 2.5 months)
  - SMA-200: Long term (about 10 months) — the most widely watched moving average globally

Interpretation:
  - Same as EMA but slower to react.
  - SMA-200 is considered the dividing line between bull and bear markets.
  - Price crossing above SMA-200 from below = major bullish signal.

Difference from EMA:
  - SMA is smoother and less prone to whipsaws but also slower to signal trend changes.
  - For NEPSE traders, EMA is generally preferred for short-term trading, SMA for long-term trend analysis.

5. Bollinger Bands (BB)
------------------------
Category: Volatility Indicator
Developed by: John Bollinger (1980s)

Definition:
Bollinger Bands consist of a middle band (SMA) with an upper and lower band set at a certain
number of standard deviations from the middle band. They expand and contract based on volatility.

Formula:
  Middle Band = SMA(20)
  Upper Band = SMA(20) + (2 × Standard Deviation of price over 20 periods)
  Lower Band = SMA(20) - (2 × Standard Deviation of price over 20 periods)

  Standard Deviation = √[ Σ(Price - SMA)² / N ]

Typical Settings:
  - Period: 20 days
  - Standard Deviation Multiplier: 2
  - Some traders use (10, 1.5) for shorter timeframes or (50, 2.5) for longer-term

Interpretation:
  - Price near Upper Band: Market may be overextended; risk of pullback (but can also indicate strength).
  - Price near Lower Band: Market may be oversold; risk of bounce (but can also indicate weakness).
  - Band Squeeze: Bands contract tightly → period of low volatility → often precedes a sharp breakout.
  - Band Expansion: Bands widen → high volatility → often after a breakout or during news events.
  - "Walking the Band": In strong trends, price can ride along the upper or lower band for extended periods.
  - BB %B: Measures where price is relative to the bands. %B = (Price - Lower) / (Upper - Lower).
    - %B > 1.0 = above upper band; %B < 0.0 = below lower band; %B = 0.5 = at middle band.

How Traders Use Bollinger Bands:
  - Mean Reversion: Buy at lower band, sell at upper band (works best in ranging markets).
  - Breakout Trading: When bands squeeze and then expand, trade in the direction of the breakout.
  - Trend Confirmation: Price consistently above the middle band = uptrend; below = downtrend.

Example:
  If NABIL's upper BB is at 540 and price is at 538 (94% BB position), the stock is near the
  upper band. Combined with an RSI of 63 (not yet overbought), it suggests upward momentum but
  watch for potential resistance at the upper band.

NEPSE Edge Cases:
  - Daily circuit limits cause prolonged narrow ranges followed by sudden limit-up/down moves.
  - Thinly traded stocks may "jump" between bands without real liquidity.
  - News-driven spikes (bonus announcements) can cause price to walk the upper band for many days.

6. Bollinger Band Width (BBW)
------------------------------
Category: Volatility Indicator

Definition:
BBW quantifies the distance between the upper and lower Bollinger Bands relative to the
middle band. It is used to identify periods of low and high volatility.

Formula:
  BBW = (Upper Band - Lower Band) / Middle Band

Interpretation:
  - Low BBW: Low volatility, consolidation phase. Often a precursor to a significant price move.
  - High BBW: High volatility, often during or after strong trends or news events.
  - BBW at historical lows for that stock: "Squeeze" setup — expect a breakout soon.

NEPSE Edge Cases:
  - BBW can remain low for extended periods in illiquid stocks stuck in narrow ranges.
  - Circuit-limit moves can cause BBW to spike temporarily without establishing a durable trend.

7. ATR (Average True Range)
-----------------------------
Category: Volatility Indicator
Developed by: J. Welles Wilder Jr. (1978)

Definition:
ATR measures market volatility by capturing the full range of price movement, including gaps.
It does NOT indicate trend direction — only the degree of price movement.

Formula:
  True Range (TR) = Maximum of:
    1. High - Low (current day's range)
    2. |High - Previous Close| (gap up measurement)
    3. |Low - Previous Close| (gap down measurement)

  ATR = Moving average of TR over N periods (typically 14 days)
  ATR = [(Previous ATR × 13) + Current TR] / 14 (Wilder's smoothing)

Typical Settings:
  - Period: 14 days (standard)
  - Some traders use 10 or 20 periods

Interpretation:
  - Rising ATR: Increasing volatility; larger potential daily price swings.
  - Falling ATR: Decreasing volatility; smaller daily moves; possible consolidation.
  - ATR does NOT indicate direction — a stock can have high ATR while moving up, down, or sideways.

How Traders Use ATR:
  - Stop-loss placement: Set stop-loss at entry price ± (1.5 to 2 × ATR) for dynamic risk management.
  - Position sizing: Use ATR to normalize position sizes across different stocks.
  - Breakout confirmation: If a breakout is accompanied by ATR expansion, it's more likely to be genuine.
  - Chandelier Exit: A trailing stop based on ATR — e.g., exit if price drops 3 × ATR from highest close.

Example:
  If NABIL has ATR(14) = 15.5, it means the stock typically moves about NPR 15.5 per day.
  A reasonable stop-loss might be 2 × ATR = NPR 31 below entry price.

NEPSE Edge Cases:
  - Circuit limits (±10%) can inflate ATR temporarily on limit-up/limit-down days.
  - Illiquid stocks with sporadic trades can show artificially extreme ATR values.
  - On stocks that barely trade, ATR can become very small, making ATR-based stops unrealistically tight.

8. OBV (On-Balance Volume)
----------------------------
Category: Volume Indicator
Developed by: Joe Granville (1963)

Definition:
OBV is a cumulative volume-based indicator that adds volume on up days and subtracts volume
on down days. It helps identify whether volume is flowing into or out of a stock.

Formula:
  If Close_today > Close_yesterday → OBV = Previous OBV + Volume_today
  If Close_today < Close_yesterday → OBV = Previous OBV - Volume_today
  If Close_today = Close_yesterday → OBV = Previous OBV (unchanged)

Interpretation:
  - Rising OBV: Buying pressure; volume confirming an uptrend.
  - Falling OBV: Selling pressure; volume confirming a downtrend.
  - OBV-Price Divergence:
    - Price falling but OBV rising → hidden accumulation (smart money buying); potential bullish reversal.
    - Price rising but OBV falling → hidden distribution (smart money selling); potential bearish reversal.
  - OBV breakout: OBV breaking above its own resistance can precede a price breakout.

How Traders Use OBV:
  - Confirm trends: Ensure price trend is supported by volume (OBV should trend in same direction).
  - Spot divergences: Look for OBV diverging from price for early reversal signals.
  - Breakout confirmation: If price breaks above resistance AND OBV is at new highs, breakout is more reliable.

Example:
  If NABIL's price has been flat at 535-540 for a week but OBV is steadily rising, it suggests
  buyers are accumulating shares quietly. A price breakout above 540 could follow.

NEPSE Edge Cases:
  - Low daily volume in many NEPSE stocks means a single large trade can cause big OBV jumps.
  - Right shares, bonus shares, and promoter block trades produce unusual volume spikes that distort OBV.
  - When a stock is frozen at circuit limit with no trades, OBV stays flat despite latent demand/supply.

9. VWAP (Volume Weighted Average Price)
-----------------------------------------
Category: Volume / Price Indicator

Definition:
VWAP is the average price of a stock during a trading session, weighted by volume.
It represents the true average price that shares were traded at and is widely used as
a benchmark by institutional traders.

Formula:
  VWAP = Σ(Price × Volume) / Σ(Volume)

  For each transaction:
  - Multiply the price by the number of shares traded
  - Sum all these values
  - Divide by total volume for the period

  Approximation using OHLCV data:
  Typical Price = (High + Low + Close) / 3
  VWAP ≈ Σ(Typical Price × Volume) / Σ(Volume) [calculated cumulatively throughout the day]

Interpretation:
  - Price above VWAP: Bullish intraday bias; buyers paying a premium relative to the average.
  - Price below VWAP: Bearish intraday bias; sellers dominating.
  - VWAP as support/resistance: Price often bounces off or reverses at the VWAP level.

How Traders Use VWAP:
  - Institutional execution: Large orders are benchmarked against VWAP to evaluate execution quality.
  - Intraday trend: Use VWAP as a dynamic support/resistance line.
  - End-of-day analysis: If a stock closes above VWAP, institutional buyers likely dominated that session.

NEPSE Edge Cases:
  - Not all NEPSE data feeds expose tick-level data; approximated VWAP from OHLC loses accuracy.
  - In illiquid stocks with few trades, VWAP can be skewed by a single large order.
  - Circuit-limit days show one-sided trading; VWAP near the limit price is not meaningful.

10. Stochastic Oscillator
--------------------------
Category: Momentum Indicator
Developed by: George Lane (1950s)

Definition:
The Stochastic Oscillator compares a stock's closing price to its price range over a specified
period. It measures momentum by determining where the current close sits within the recent
high-low range. The idea is that in uptrends, prices tend to close near the high; in downtrends,
near the low.

Formula:
  %K = [(Current Close - Lowest Low) / (Highest High - Lowest Low)] × 100
  %D = 3-period Simple Moving Average of %K

  Where "Lowest Low" and "Highest High" are calculated over N periods.

Typical Settings:
  - (14, 3, 3): N = 14 periods, %K smoothing = 3, %D = 3-period SMA of %K
  - Fast Stochastic: Uses raw %K (more sensitive, more noise)
  - Slow Stochastic: Uses smoothed %K (standard, less noise)

Interpretation:
  - Overbought: %K or %D above 80 — price near the top of recent range.
  - Oversold: %K or %D below 20 — price near the bottom of recent range.
  - Bullish signal: %K crosses above %D in the oversold region (below 20).
  - Bearish signal: %K crosses below %D in the overbought region (above 80).
  - Stochastic Divergence: Similar to RSI divergence but using %K line.

How Traders Use It:
  - Combine with support/resistance: Buy when Stochastic is oversold AND price is at support.
  - Confirm RSI: Stochastic oversold + RSI oversold = stronger buy signal.
  - Avoid in strong trends: Stochastic works best in range-bound markets; in trending markets, it can give premature signals.

NEPSE Edge Cases:
  - In trending NEPSE stocks, the oscillator can remain overbought/oversold for long periods.
  - Illiquid stocks with large gaps can make %K swing wildly on low volume.
  - Daily circuit limits can cause the indicator to pin at extremes for consecutive days.

11. ADX (Average Directional Index)
-------------------------------------
Category: Trend Strength Indicator
Developed by: J. Welles Wilder Jr. (1978)

Definition:
ADX measures the strength of a trend, regardless of its direction. It does not tell you
whether the trend is up or down — only how strong it is. It is derived from the Directional
Movement Index (DMI) system.

Formula:
  +DM (Plus Directional Movement) = Current High - Previous High (if positive and > -DM, else 0)
  -DM (Minus Directional Movement) = Previous Low - Current Low (if positive and > +DM, else 0)

  +DI = 100 × Smoothed +DM / ATR (14-period)
  -DI = 100 × Smoothed -DM / ATR (14-period)

  DX = |+DI - (-DI)| / (+DI + (-DI)) × 100
  ADX = 14-period smoothed average of DX

Typical Settings:
  - Period: 14 (standard)
  - Range: 0 to 100

Interpretation:
  - ADX < 20: Weak or no trend; market is ranging/choppy. Avoid trend-following strategies.
  - ADX 20-25: Trend may be emerging; prepare for potential breakout.
  - ADX 25-50: Strong trend in place; trend-following strategies work well.
  - ADX 50-75: Very strong trend; consider trailing stops to protect profits.
  - ADX > 75: Extremely strong trend; rare but powerful. Watch for exhaustion.
  - +DI above -DI: The trend is bullish (upward direction).
  - -DI above +DI: The trend is bearish (downward direction).

How Traders Use ADX:
  - Trend filter: Only take trend-following trades when ADX > 25.
  - Combine with DI crossovers: Buy when +DI crosses above -DI AND ADX > 20.
  - Avoid mean-reversion trades: When ADX is high, don't try to fade the trend.
  - ADX turning down from high levels: Trend is losing strength; prepare for potential reversal.

Example:
  If NABIL has ADX = 35 with +DI = 28 and -DI = 15, it indicates a strong bullish trend.
  If ADX was 12 last month and is now 35, the trend has significantly strengthened.

NEPSE Edge Cases:
  - Many NEPSE stocks trade in narrow ranges for long periods, keeping ADX below 20 most of the time.
  - Sudden news-driven moves can cause ADX to spike quickly but not sustain, leading to false signals.
  - Low liquidity stocks may show erratic +DI/-DI readings that don't reflect genuine trends.

12. CCI (Commodity Channel Index)
-----------------------------------
Category: Momentum Indicator
Developed by: Donald Lambert (1980)

Definition:
CCI measures a stock's price deviation from its statistical average. Despite its name, it is
widely used for stocks, not just commodities. It identifies cyclical trends and overbought/oversold levels.

Formula:
  Typical Price (TP) = (High + Low + Close) / 3
  CCI = (TP - SMA(TP, N)) / (0.015 × Mean Deviation)

  Mean Deviation = Average of |TP - SMA(TP, N)| over N periods
  The 0.015 constant scales CCI so that ~75% of values fall between -100 and +100.

Typical Settings:
  - Period: 20 (standard)
  - Range: Unbounded (can go well above +100 or below -100)

Interpretation:
  - CCI > +100: Stock is in a strong uptrend; potential overbought condition.
  - CCI < -100: Stock is in a strong downtrend; potential oversold condition.
  - CCI crossing above +100 from below: Potential buy signal (new uptrend beginning).
  - CCI crossing below -100 from above: Potential sell signal (new downtrend beginning).
  - CCI returning to zero: Trend losing momentum; price returning to average.

How Traders Use CCI:
  - Trend identification: CCI > 0 = bullish bias; CCI < 0 = bearish bias.
  - Overbought/oversold: Use +100/-100 levels similar to RSI's 70/30 levels.
  - Divergence: Price making new highs but CCI not confirming = potential reversal.

13. Williams %R (Williams Percent Range)
------------------------------------------
Category: Momentum Indicator
Developed by: Larry Williams

Definition:
Williams %R is similar to the Stochastic Oscillator but inverted. It measures where the
current close is relative to the highest high over a lookback period. It oscillates between 0 and -100.

Formula:
  %R = [(Highest High - Close) / (Highest High - Lowest Low)] × (-100)

  Over N periods (typically 14).

Interpretation:
  - %R between 0 and -20: Overbought — price near the top of its recent range.
  - %R between -80 and -100: Oversold — price near the bottom of its recent range.
  - %R crossing above -80 from below: Potential bullish reversal signal.
  - %R crossing below -20 from above: Potential bearish reversal signal.

How Traders Use Williams %R:
  - Very similar to Stochastic Oscillator usage but with inverted scale.
  - Best used in conjunction with trend indicators (EMA, ADX) to filter signals.
  - In trending markets, %R can stay overbought or oversold for extended periods.

14. ROC (Rate of Change)
--------------------------
Category: Momentum Indicator

Definition:
ROC measures the percentage change in price between the current price and the price N periods ago.
It is one of the simplest momentum indicators.

Formula:
  ROC = [(Close_today - Close_N_periods_ago) / Close_N_periods_ago] × 100

Typical Settings:
  - Period: 12 or 14 days for short-term; 25 for medium-term; 200 for long-term

Interpretation:
  - ROC > 0: Price is higher than N periods ago; positive momentum.
  - ROC < 0: Price is lower than N periods ago; negative momentum.
  - ROC increasing: Momentum accelerating.
  - ROC decreasing (even if still positive): Momentum decelerating; potential trend weakening.
  - ROC divergence: Price making new highs but ROC declining = bearish divergence.

How Traders Use ROC:
  - Momentum screening: Filter stocks with highest positive ROC for momentum investing.
  - Overbought/oversold: Extreme ROC values relative to historical norms may signal reversals.
  - Trend confirmation: Positive ROC confirms uptrend; negative confirms downtrend.

15. MFI (Money Flow Index)
----------------------------
Category: Volume / Momentum Indicator

Definition:
MFI is a volume-weighted version of RSI. It incorporates both price and volume to measure
buying and selling pressure. Often called the "volume-weighted RSI."

Formula:
  Typical Price (TP) = (High + Low + Close) / 3
  Raw Money Flow = TP × Volume
  Positive Money Flow = Sum of Raw Money Flow on days where TP > Previous TP (over N periods)
  Negative Money Flow = Sum of Raw Money Flow on days where TP < Previous TP (over N periods)
  Money Flow Ratio = Positive Money Flow / Negative Money Flow
  MFI = 100 - [100 / (1 + Money Flow Ratio)]

Typical Settings:
  - Period: 14 days
  - Range: 0 to 100

Interpretation:
  - MFI > 80: Overbought — strong buying pressure may be exhausting.
  - MFI < 20: Oversold — strong selling pressure may be exhausting.
  - MFI divergence with price: Similar to RSI divergence but volume-weighted, often more reliable.
  - MFI is generally considered more reliable than RSI alone because it accounts for volume.

NEPSE Edge Cases:
  - Low-volume NEPSE stocks can have unreliable MFI readings because a few trades dominate.
  - On days with very low volume, MFI can swing wildly and produce false signals.

16. Accumulation/Distribution Line (A/D Line)
-----------------------------------------------
Category: Volume Indicator
Developed by: Marc Chaikin

Definition:
The A/D Line measures the cumulative flow of money into and out of a stock using the relationship
between the close and the high-low range, weighted by volume. It helps determine whether a
stock is being accumulated (bought) or distributed (sold).

Formula:
  Money Flow Multiplier (MFM) = [(Close - Low) - (High - Close)] / (High - Low)
  Money Flow Volume = MFM × Volume
  A/D Line = Previous A/D + Money Flow Volume

  When Close = High: MFM = +1 (all volume is buying pressure)
  When Close = Low: MFM = -1 (all volume is selling pressure)
  When Close = Midpoint: MFM = 0 (balanced)

Interpretation:
  - Rising A/D Line: Accumulation — money flowing into the stock; bullish.
  - Falling A/D Line: Distribution — money flowing out of the stock; bearish.
  - A/D Line diverging from price:
    - Price rising but A/D falling → distribution; potential top forming.
    - Price falling but A/D rising → accumulation; potential bottom forming.

17. CMF (Chaikin Money Flow)
------------------------------
Category: Volume Indicator
Developed by: Marc Chaikin

Definition:
CMF measures the amount of Money Flow Volume over a specific period. It is derived from
the A/D Line concept but expressed as a bounded oscillator.

Formula:
  CMF = Sum of Money Flow Volume over N periods / Sum of Volume over N periods

  Where Money Flow Volume = MFM × Volume (same MFM as A/D Line)

Typical Settings:
  - Period: 20 or 21 days
  - Range: -1 to +1

Interpretation:
  - CMF > 0: Buying pressure dominates; bullish.
  - CMF < 0: Selling pressure dominates; bearish.
  - CMF rising while price rises: Strong uptrend confirmed by volume.
  - CMF falling while price rises: Potential weakness; distribution may be underway.

18. Ichimoku Cloud (Ichimoku Kinko Hyo)
-----------------------------------------
Category: Trend / Support-Resistance Indicator
Developed by: Goichi Hosoda (1930s, published 1969)

Definition:
Ichimoku Cloud is a comprehensive indicator that defines support/resistance, identifies trend
direction, gauges momentum, and provides trading signals — all in one view. "Ichimoku Kinko Hyo"
translates to "one glance equilibrium chart."

Components and Formulas:
  Tenkan-sen (Conversion Line) = (Highest High + Lowest Low) / 2 over 9 periods
  Kijun-sen (Base Line) = (Highest High + Lowest Low) / 2 over 26 periods
  Senkou Span A (Leading Span A) = (Tenkan-sen + Kijun-sen) / 2, plotted 26 periods ahead
  Senkou Span B (Leading Span B) = (Highest High + Lowest Low) / 2 over 52 periods, plotted 26 periods ahead
  Chikou Span (Lagging Span) = Current Close, plotted 26 periods behind

  The "Cloud" (Kumo) is the area between Senkou Span A and Senkou Span B.

Interpretation:
  - Price above the Cloud: Bullish trend. The cloud acts as support.
  - Price below the Cloud: Bearish trend. The cloud acts as resistance.
  - Price inside the Cloud: Trend is indeterminate; market is consolidating.
  - Cloud color: When Senkou Span A > B, cloud is bullish (often green). When B > A, bearish (often red).
  - Cloud thickness: Thicker cloud = stronger support/resistance.
  - Tenkan-sen / Kijun-sen Cross (TK Cross):
    - Tenkan crosses above Kijun = bullish signal (above cloud = strong, inside cloud = neutral, below = weak).
    - Tenkan crosses below Kijun = bearish signal.
  - Chikou Span: If above price from 26 periods ago = bullish; below = bearish.

How Traders Use Ichimoku:
  - Full system: All five lines must align for a strong signal.
  - Quick trend check: Is price above or below the cloud?
  - Cloud as support/resistance: Enter on bounces off the cloud edge.
  - Future cloud: The cloud plotted 26 periods ahead gives a forward-looking support/resistance view.

NEPSE Edge Cases:
  - Ichimoku was designed for Japanese stock markets with 6-day trading weeks; the default settings
    (9, 26, 52) may need adjustment for NEPSE's 5-day week (some use 7, 22, 44 instead).
  - Illiquid NEPSE stocks often have thin or nonexistent clouds, making signals less reliable.
  - The 26-period forward projection of the cloud can be misleading for stocks with sudden fundamental changes.

19. Pivot Points
-----------------
Category: Support & Resistance Indicator

Definition:
Pivot Points are calculated price levels used to identify potential support and resistance
zones for the next trading session. They are derived from the previous session's high, low,
and close prices.

Formula (Standard Pivot Points):
  Pivot Point (PP) = (High + Low + Close) / 3
  First Resistance (R1) = (2 × PP) - Low
  First Support (S1) = (2 × PP) - High
  Second Resistance (R2) = PP + (High - Low)
  Second Support (S2) = PP - (High - Low)
  Third Resistance (R3) = High + 2 × (PP - Low)
  Third Support (S3) = Low - 2 × (High - PP)

Other Methods:
  - Fibonacci Pivot Points: Use Fibonacci ratios (38.2%, 61.8%) of the High-Low range.
  - Woodie's Pivot Points: Give more weight to the closing price.
  - Camarilla Pivot Points: Use a narrower range, providing more levels closer to the current price.

Interpretation:
  - Price above PP: Bullish bias for the session.
  - Price below PP: Bearish bias for the session.
  - R1, R2, R3: Expected resistance levels where selling pressure may increase.
  - S1, S2, S3: Expected support levels where buying pressure may increase.

How Traders Use Pivot Points:
  - Intraday trading: Use as entry/exit levels for day trades.
  - Set targets: Take profit at the next resistance level; set stop-loss below the nearest support.
  - Confluence: Pivot levels that align with other indicators (EMA, Fibonacci) are stronger.

20. Fibonacci Retracement
---------------------------
Category: Support & Resistance Indicator

Definition:
Fibonacci Retracement levels are horizontal lines that indicate where support and resistance
are likely to occur. They are based on the Fibonacci sequence ratios and are used to identify
potential reversal levels during a pullback within a trend.

Key Fibonacci Levels:
  - 23.6% retracement
  - 38.2% retracement
  - 50.0% retracement (not a true Fibonacci number but widely used)
  - 61.8% retracement (the "golden ratio")
  - 78.6% retracement (square root of 61.8%)

Formula:
  For an uptrend (measuring retracement from A=low to B=high):
  Retracement Level = B - [(B - A) × Fibonacci Ratio]

  Example: If a stock moves from NPR 400 (A) to NPR 500 (B):
  - 23.6% level = 500 - (100 × 0.236) = NPR 476.4
  - 38.2% level = 500 - (100 × 0.382) = NPR 461.8
  - 50.0% level = 500 - (100 × 0.500) = NPR 450.0
  - 61.8% level = 500 - (100 × 0.618) = NPR 438.2

Interpretation:
  - 38.2% retracement: Shallow pullback; strong trend likely to continue.
  - 50.0% retracement: Moderate pullback; common reversal zone.
  - 61.8% retracement: Deep pullback; if this level holds, trend may resume; if broken, trend may have reversed.

How Traders Use Fibonacci Retracement:
  - Identify buy zones: After a rally, wait for a pullback to a Fibonacci level before entering.
  - Set stop-losses: Place stop-loss just below the next Fibonacci level.
  - Combine with other indicators: A Fibonacci level that coincides with an EMA or pivot point creates
    a "confluence zone" — a stronger support/resistance level.

NEPSE Edge Cases:
  - Works best for stocks with clear trend moves (like commercial banks during rally phases).
  - Less reliable for thinly traded stocks where price movements are driven by single large orders.
  - On NEPSE, the 50% and 61.8% levels tend to be the most respected retracement zones.

21. Keltner Channels
---------------------
Category: Volatility Indicator
Developed by: Chester Keltner (1960s), modernized by Linda Raschke

Definition:
Keltner Channels are volatility-based bands set above and below an EMA. They are similar to
Bollinger Bands but use ATR instead of standard deviation for band width.

Formula:
  Middle Line = EMA(20)
  Upper Channel = EMA(20) + (2 × ATR(10))
  Lower Channel = EMA(20) - (2 × ATR(10))

Interpretation:
  - Price above upper channel: Strong uptrend; overbought but may continue trending.
  - Price below lower channel: Strong downtrend; oversold but may continue trending.
  - Price within channels: Normal trading range.
  - Channel breakout: Price closing outside the channels signals a potential trend move.

Difference from Bollinger Bands:
  - Bollinger Bands use standard deviation (price-based); Keltner uses ATR (volatility-based).
  - Keltner Channels are smoother and less reactive to single-day spikes.
  - TTM Squeeze: When Bollinger Bands contract INSIDE Keltner Channels, it signals extreme low volatility
    and an impending large move.

22. Parabolic SAR (Stop and Reverse)
--------------------------------------
Category: Trend / Trailing Stop Indicator
Developed by: J. Welles Wilder Jr. (1978)

Definition:
Parabolic SAR provides potential entry and exit points by placing dots above or below the price.
When dots are below the price, it suggests an uptrend; when above, a downtrend. "SAR" stands
for "Stop and Reverse."

Formula:
  SAR_next = SAR_current + AF × (EP - SAR_current)

  Where:
  - AF (Acceleration Factor) starts at 0.02 and increases by 0.02 each time a new EP is made, up to 0.20.
  - EP (Extreme Point) is the highest high in an uptrend or lowest low in a downtrend.

Interpretation:
  - Dots below price: Uptrend in effect; use SAR as a trailing stop.
  - Dots above price: Downtrend in effect; use SAR as a trailing stop.
  - Dots flipping from below to above price: Sell signal (trend reversal to downtrend).
  - Dots flipping from above to below price: Buy signal (trend reversal to uptrend).

How Traders Use Parabolic SAR:
  - Trailing stop-loss: Use the SAR dot as a dynamic stop-loss level.
  - Trend identification: Quick visual indicator of current trend direction.
  - Combined with ADX: Use Parabolic SAR only when ADX > 25 (strong trend) to avoid whipsaws in ranging markets.

NEPSE Edge Cases:
  - In choppy, range-bound NEPSE stocks, Parabolic SAR generates many false reversals.
  - Best used on trending stocks (commercial banks during rally phases, hydropower during sector rotation).
  - The acceleration factor can cause SAR to catch up to price too quickly in volatile NEPSE stocks.

23. Beta (Market Sensitivity)
-------------------------------
Category: Market Sensitivity Indicator

Definition:
Beta measures a stock's volatility relative to the overall market (NEPSE Index). It indicates
how much a stock's price tends to move when the market moves.

Formula:
  Beta = Covariance(Stock Returns, Market Returns) / Variance(Market Returns)

  In simpler terms: Beta is the slope of the regression line when plotting stock returns (y-axis)
  against market returns (x-axis).

Typical Values:
  - Beta > 1: Stock is more volatile than the market. If NEPSE rises 1%, the stock tends to rise more than 1%.
  - Beta ≈ 1: Stock moves roughly in line with the market.
  - Beta < 1: Stock is less volatile than the market (defensive stock).
  - Beta < 0: Stock tends to move inversely to the market (extremely rare on NEPSE).

How Traders Use Beta:
  - Portfolio construction: Mix high-beta and low-beta stocks for desired risk level.
  - Market timing: In bull markets, prefer high-beta stocks for amplified gains.
    In bear markets, prefer low-beta stocks for capital preservation.
  - Sector analysis: On NEPSE, commercial banks typically have Beta close to 1;
    hydropower stocks and microfinance can have Beta > 1.5.

Example:
  If NABIL has Beta = 0.85, it means when NEPSE Index rises 1%, NABIL typically rises about 0.85%.
  If UPPER (a hydropower stock) has Beta = 1.6, it would typically move 1.6% for every 1% move in NEPSE.

NEPSE Edge Cases:
  - Many stocks have limited history or long periods of illiquidity, making Beta unstable.
  - Sector concentration in NEPSE (heavy bank weight) can inflate correlations and Beta values.
  - Regulatory changes and sudden sentiment shifts can quickly change a stock's Beta.

24. Percentage Price Oscillator (PPO)
---------------------------------------
Category: Momentum Indicator

Definition:
PPO shows the percentage difference between two EMAs. It is similar to MACD but expressed in
percentage terms, making it easier to compare across different stocks.

Formula:
  PPO = [(EMA(12) - EMA(26)) / EMA(26)] × 100
  Signal Line = 9-period EMA of PPO
  PPO Histogram = PPO - Signal Line

Interpretation:
  - Same signals as MACD but normalized as percentages.
  - PPO > 0: Short-term EMA above long-term EMA; bullish.
  - PPO < 0: Short-term EMA below long-term EMA; bearish.
  - Advantage over MACD: Since PPO is in percentage terms, you can compare PPO values across
    different stocks regardless of their price levels.

25. Volume Profile / Volume Analysis
--------------------------------------
Category: Volume Indicator

Definition:
Volume analysis examines trading volume patterns to confirm trends, identify reversals,
and gauge the strength of price moves. While not a single formula, understanding volume
context is essential for NEPSE traders.

Key Volume Concepts:
  - Volume Confirmation: Price movements on high volume are more significant than on low volume.
  - Volume Climax: Extremely high volume after a prolonged move can signal exhaustion (trend reversal).
  - Low Volume Pullbacks: Pullbacks on declining volume suggest the trend is intact (healthy correction).
  - High Volume Breakouts: Breakouts above resistance on high volume are more likely to sustain.
  - Average Volume: Compare current volume to the 20-day or 50-day average volume.

NEPSE-Specific Volume Interpretation:
  - Many NEPSE stocks have very low average daily volume (< 5,000 shares). What constitutes "high volume"
    varies dramatically between stocks.
  - Promoter share transactions and block deals can create one-day volume spikes that don't reflect
    genuine retail/institutional interest.
  - On right share listing days and bonus adjustment days, volume can spike artificially.
  - The NEPSE trading session (11 AM to 3 PM NST) is short; volume patterns should be interpreted
    within this compressed timeframe.

Combining Indicators — Best Practices for NEPSE
-------------------------------------------------

No single indicator is sufficient for making trading decisions. Here are proven combinations:

1. Trend + Momentum:
   - EMA-20/50 for trend direction + RSI for entry timing.
   - ADX for trend strength + MACD for momentum direction.

2. Volatility + Momentum:
   - Bollinger Band Squeeze + RSI oversold/overbought for breakout entries.
   - ATR for stop-loss sizing + Stochastic for entry timing.

3. Volume + Price:
   - OBV divergence + price breakout for confirmation.
   - VWAP + volume spike for intraday institutional activity detection.

4. Complete System:
   - Use ADX to determine if market is trending (> 25) or ranging (< 20).
   - In trending markets: EMA crossovers + MACD + OBV confirmation.
   - In ranging markets: RSI/Stochastic overbought/oversold + Bollinger Band bounces.

Golden Rules for NEPSE Technical Analysis:
  - Always confirm signals with volume — low-volume signals on NEPSE are unreliable.
  - Account for circuit limits — indicators can be distorted by ±10% daily price bands.
  - Be cautious with illiquid stocks — most indicators assume reasonable liquidity.
  - Corporate actions (bonus, rights, splits) can distort historical data and indicator values.
  - NEPSE trades only ~220 days per year; adjust period settings accordingly.
  - Use multiple timeframes — daily for trend, weekly for confirmation, monthly for big picture.

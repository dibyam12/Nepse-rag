SECTION 1: WHAT IS FUNDAMENTAL ANALYSIS IN NEPSE CONTEXT?
=============================================================================

Fundamental analysis evaluates a company's financial health, profitability,
and valuation to determine its intrinsic worth. In NEPSE, it focuses on:

1. EARNINGS POWER — How much profit does the company generate?
2. GROWTH — Is profit growing quarter over quarter / year over year?
3. VALUATION — Is the stock priced fairly relative to earnings?
4. ASSET QUALITY — For banks: how clean is the loan book?
5. CAPITAL ADEQUACY — For banks: does the bank meet NRB requirements?
6. DIVIDEND HISTORY — What is the track record of cash/stock dividends?

Unlike technical analysis (which times entries and exits), fundamental
analysis answers: "Is this company worth owning for the long term?"

Nepal Rastra Bank (NRB) is the central bank and primary regulator of Nepal's
banking sector. SEBON (Securities Board of Nepal) regulates the securities market.

SECTION 2: KEY FUNDAMENTAL METRICS AND HOW TO INTERPRET THEM
=============================================================================

--- EPS (Earnings Per Share) ---
Formula: Net Profit ÷ Total Outstanding Shares
What it means: Profit earned per share. Higher is better.
NEPSE Context: Strong EPS for commercial banks is Rs 20+. 
Banks below Rs 10 EPS are considered weak performers.
NEPSE Banking Average (Q2 FY2082/83): ~Rs 17
Best in class: NABIL (Rs 35.18), EBL (Rs 30.86), SCB (Rs 27.35)

--- P/E Ratio (Price to Earnings) ---
Formula: LTP ÷ Annualized EPS
What it means: How many rupees investors pay for Rs 1 of earnings.
NEPSE Context:
  - Commercial banks sector average P/E: 15.80
  - NEPSE overall P/E: 38.32 (as of late 2025)
  - Global healthy range: 15-20
  - P/E below 12 may indicate undervaluation (or weak earnings expectations)
  - P/E above 40 suggests overvaluation or very low current earnings
Important: Very high P/E (e.g., NICA at 190) usually means EPS is nearly zero,
not that the stock is exceptionally valuable.

--- ROE (Return on Equity) ---
Formula: Net Profit ÷ Total Shareholders' Equity × 100
What it means: How efficiently management uses shareholder capital.
NEPSE Context: ROE above 15% is strong. Below 5% is weak.
Strong banks in Nepal: NABIL (14.93%), EBL (13.13%), SCB (12.96%)

--- NAV / Book Value Per Share ---
Formula: Total Equity ÷ Total Outstanding Shares
What it means: The theoretical per-share value of the company's net assets.
NEPSE Context: If LTP is significantly above NAV, the stock may be overvalued.
P/B = LTP ÷ NAV. NEPSE banking sector average P/B: 1.51 (reasonably valued).

--- NPM (Net Profit Margin) ---
Formula: Net Profit ÷ Total Revenue × 100
What it means: Percentage of revenue that becomes profit.
NEPSE Context: Strong NPM for banks is above 35%.
Best: NABIL (44.56%), SCB (44.58%), EBL (40.31%)

--- NPL Ratio (Non-Performing Loan Ratio) ---
Formula: Non-Performing Loans ÷ Total Loans × 100
What it means: Percentage of loans that borrowers are not repaying.
NEPSE Context: Lower is better. NRB regulatory limit: 5%.
Excellent: EBL (0.68%), NABIL (3.37%)
Warning zone: Above 5% — regulatory concern

--- Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR) ---
Formula: Capital ÷ Risk-Weighted Assets × 100
What it means: Regulatory requirement to absorb losses.
NRB Minimum: 11% for commercial banks
Banking sector average: 12.61%

--- Dividend Yield ---
Formula: (Dividend per Share ÷ LTP) × 100
What it means: Annual return from dividends alone.
NEPSE banks typically distribute cash dividends of 10-30% of face value (Rs 100).
NABIL declared 12.5% cash dividend for FY2081/82 = Rs 12.50 per share.
At LTP of 530, yield = 12.50/530 = 2.36%

--- Distributable Profit ---
The amount of profit legally available to distribute as dividends,
after regulatory deductions (loan loss provisions, capital reserve requirements).
A high distributable profit is a positive indicator for dividend prospects.
NABIL Q3 FY2082/83 distributable profit: Rs 4.37 Billion (+52.17% YoY)

SECTION 7: HOW TO IDENTIFY OVER/UNDERVALUATION IN NEPSE
=============================================================================

Step 1 — Compare P/E to sector average
  - Stock P/E below sector average = potentially undervalued
  - Stock P/E above sector average = potentially overvalued or high-growth expected

Step 2 — Compare P/B to 1.0
  - P/B below 1.0 = trading below book value (potential deep value)
  - P/B 1.0–2.0 = fairly valued for banks
  - P/B above 3.0 = premium pricing, requires strong ROE justification

Step 3 — ROE vs Cost of Equity
  - If ROE > 12%, stock may justify a P/B above 1.5
  - If ROE < 5%, even low P/B may not be attractive

Step 4 — EPS Growth Trend
  - Compare current EPS to same quarter last year (YoY growth)
  - Consistent 15%+ EPS growth = strong quality signal

Step 5 — NPL Trend (for banks)
  - Declining NPL ratio = improving asset quality = positive
  - Rising NPL = credit risk concern = negative signal
